Arcade Shooters (Run & Gun): Fast-paced, with little emphasis on realism. Health often regenerates, movement is fluid, and action is constant.
Examples: Doom (2016), Call of Duty, Serious Sam.

Tactical Shooters: Slower, more methodical gameplay. Low time-to-kill (TTK), limited HUD, and heavy emphasis on teamwork, positioning, and strategy. One bullet can kill.
Examples: Rainbow Six Siege, Escape from Tarkov, ARMA 3.

Hero Shooters: A subgenre where players choose from a roster of unique characters ("heroes"), each with distinct abilities, weapons, and roles (tank, damage, support). Team composition is key.
Examples: Overwatch, Team Fortress 2, Valorant (hybrid with tactical shooter).

Battle Royale: A large-scale last-man-standing format. Players drop into a map, scavenge for weapons and gear, and fight until only one player or team remains. The play area shrinks over time.
Examples: Fortnite, Apex Legends, Warzone.

Shooters with Mechanics:

Cover-Based Shooters (TPS): Peeking from behind walls and barriers (Gears of War, Uncharted).

Looter Shooters: Combining shooting with RPG loot systems—grinding for randomized weapon stats (Destiny 2, Borderlands, The Division 2).

Boomer Shooters: Retro-style FPS games inspired by 90s classics—fast movement, no reloading, mazes of enemies (Prodeus, Dusk, Turbo Overkill).

Core Mechanics
Hit-scan vs. Projectile: Hit-scan weapons (most rifles) register instantly when fired. Projectile weapons (rockets, arrows) have travel time and require leading the target.

Aiming Down Sights (ADS): Using iron sights or scopes for better accuracy at the cost of mobility.

Movement: Many shooters feature sliding, dashing, wall-running, or bunny-hopping to increase skill ceilings (e.g., Titanfall 2, Apex Legends).

Recoil Control: Managing weapon kickback to keep shots on target.

Map Control & Spawns: Especially in arena shooters (Quake, Halo), controlling power-ups and weapon spawn points is more important than pure aim.

History & Evolution
Early Days (1990s): Wolfenstein 3D (1992) and Doom (1993) popularized the FPS. GoldenEye 007 (1997) brought split-screen multiplayer to consoles.

Online Revolution (Late 90s–00s): Quake and Counter-Strike established online PC competitive play. Halo: Combat Evolved (2001) perfected console controls with "aim assist."

Modern Warfare Era (2007+): Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare introduced progression systems, create-a-class, and killstreaks—mechanics now copied by nearly all shooters.

Live Service (2010s+): Games shifted to "games as a service"—seasonal updates, battle passes, and microtransactions (Overwatch 2, Fortnite, Destiny 2).

Why Are Shooters So Popular?
Instant feedback loop: Aim, shoot, hit/kill—simple to understand but hard to master.

High skill ceiling: Mechanical aim, movement, game sense, and teamwork create endless depth.

Social play: Team-based shooters are perfect for co-op or competitive multiplayer with friends.

Adrenaline & flow state: Fast decision-making under pressure is exhilarating.
